In [_The Steep Cost of Capture_](FIXME), [Meredith Whittaker](FIXME) --- NYU
professor, President of the Signal Foundation, and former head of Google's Open
Research Group --- argues that big tech money has systematically biased research
into the societal costs and benefits of LLMs. She encourages academics and tech
workers to explicitly call out this systemic bias, criticize LLMs more freely,
and organize to protect each other from backlash.

I read this article as part of an effort to re-acquaint myself with the state of
LLMs and the debates surrounding them. Meredith Whittaker's background made me
especially interested in her views, and though I found some of her logic hard to
follow, the editorial was well worth reading.

## The Argument, Briefly

Meredith advances her argument in just a few key points:

- The current LLM boom isn't the result of a scientific breakthrough. Instead,
  it's the product of decades-old machine learning algorithms paired with the
  huge computing resources, data collection, and budgets of Google, Amazon,
  Microsoft, Meta, and their peers.
- Tech giants lavishly fund research into large language models, elevating its
  academic prestige. Simultaneously, research into LLMs requires donated access
  to data sets and cloud computing. So in practice, academic success requires
  close industry ties.
- To preserve their industry relationships, researchers praise LLMs and
  ostracize critics.
- Despite being captured by industry, prominent researchers still have the
  _appearance_ of impartiality. Their support allows big tech to successfully
  lobby for government investment into LLMs. Most of this investment ends up
  back in big tech's pockets.
- To evalualate the societal costs and benefits of LLMs, we need more impartial
  academic research. Whittaker encourages academics and tech workers to
  explicitly discuss the systemic bias that tech money introduces, to criticize
  LLMs more freely, and to organize to protect each other from any backlash.

Throughout, Meredith supports these claims with examples, references, and
analogies to military investment in research during the Cold War.

Overall, I found the article's rabble-rousing tone entertaining but distracting.
Despite her harsh criticism of the status quo, Meredith isn't actually
suggesting that we abandon LLMs, ban corporate research grants, or embrace
socialism --- at least, not in this article. She's merely proposing more
skepticism during an obvious boom, more academic freedom, and more collective
bargaining for workers' rights. And while I'm generally in favor of critical
research, the article makes some unexplained leaps.

## What about OpenAI?

Set against a backdrop of pessimism about technology companies, it's easy to nod
along as Meredith attacks big tech. But the company most associated with LLMs
isn't Google, Amazon, Meta, or Microsoft --- it's OpenAI, who captured the
world's imagination with ChatGPT.

Sure, tech giants did much the early research into LLMs. But reality contradicts
the idea that the confluence of compute, data, and talent required to develop
LLMs is _only_ available at tech giants: OpenAI has done it, Anthropic has done
it, and a crop of new startups (many with ex-OpenAI founders) seems poised to do
it too. The barriers to entry are high in absolute terms, but modest relative to
the perceived market size and well within the limits of venture funds. And
critically, startups have been able to build competitive models without decades
of accumulated data.

Perhaps this doesn't change the thrust of the argument; like the US government,
perhaps venture capitalists are victims of big tech's manufactured pro-LLM
consensus. But even still, I'd expect Meredith to make this claim explicitly.
Without any mention of OpenAI or Anthropic, the article feels incomplete.

## The Bitter Lesson

I also found Meredith's portrayal of LLMs disorientingly pessimistic. Without explicitly saying
so, she paints LLMs as a cynical repackaging of old algorithms that's sucking
the attention away from more promising new research. But if this is really her
position, it's so uncharitable that it degrades my trust in the rest of her claims.
